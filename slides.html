Dialect contact, spatial variation, and language change in the morphology of the Basque auxiliary verb

Introduction

The Basque language

  • Spoken natively by ~750,000 people

Basque verbal morphology

  • Basque verbs are conjugated in two parts
    • a participial stem with the verbal root and an aspect marker (perfect, imperfect, or futurate)
    • an auxiliary verb with tense (present or past), mood and φ-agreement morphemes
    • (synthetic verbs, conjugated in one part, will be discussed later)

Basque verbal morphology

  • The verb agrees with (almost) all core arguments
    • The language has ergative case marking, so core argument = anything marked absolutive, ergative, or dative

Shape of Basque verbs

  • A finite Basque verb can have maximally one φ prefix
    • This prefix may be absent, in which case it is replaced by an expletive (T/M dependent)
    • The prefix may also be phonologically empty but syntactically present (in which case no expletive is inserted)
  • Other φ morphemes are suffixes
  • Complementizers, modal/evidential particles, etc. may be prefixes or suffixes, always more peripheral than the φ morphemes

agreement vs Agree-ment

  • There is debate about whether Basque φ morphemes are clitics or Agreement markers
    • I will use “agreement” as an empirical term and “Agree” or “Agreement” to refer to a specific syntactic operation

Examples

  • gu joan g-ara / zu joan z-ara ‘we/you went’

  • Jonek maite g-aitu-∅ / maite z-aitu-∅
    ‘J loves us/you’

  • guk Jon maite d-u-gu / zuk maite d-u-zu
    ‘we/you love J’

  • Joni sagarra eman d-i-o-gu / d-i-o-zu
    zuri eman d-i-zu-gu
    ‘J gave us/you an apple / we gave you’

Terminology

  • Traditional Basque philology/linguistics refers to verb paradigms by the case(s) that the verb agrees with
    • nor = absolutive only
    • nor-nork = absolutive + erg
    • nor-nori = abs + dat
    • nor-nori-nork = abs + dat + erg
  • I will adapt these terms, (but) using them as rigid designators of certain paradigms

Data sources

  • Primary data source: Morfologia del verbo auxiliar vasco (Yrizar ed.)
    • Records the auxiliary verb paradigm for 215 localities (mostly entire paradigms, some extinct dialects only partially)
  • 3 epochs of data collection
    • 1860s: Louis-Lucien Bonaparte
    • 1920s: Resurección María Azkue, Gerhard Bähr
    • 1980s: MVAV

Variation in Basque auxiliaries

Differential Object Marking (DOM)

  • Use of a dative suffix to mark a direct object
    • ≈ change of a nor-nork form to nor-nori-nork
    • But: also generally involves changing the case of the DO to dative
  • Restrictions based on animacy, person, and other semantic features of the noun

Example

Ni-ketxi-eiikusidi-o-t
I-erghouse-the.abssee3sg.abs-root-1sg.erg
‘I have seen the house.’
Ni-kMikel-eriikusid-o-tsa-t.
I-ergMikel-datsee3sg.abs-root-3sg.dat-1sg.erg
‘I have seen Mikel.’
(Gernika; Rodriguez Ordoñez 2017 2a,b)

History of the construction

  • Mounole (2011; 268) finds the beginnings of DOM in 18th century texts from several dialect regions
    • viz. Gipuzkoa, High Navarre, and Bizkaia (short commands only)
    • always with verbs borrowed from Spanish
  • Consensus is that it is a loan (or calque) from Spanish
  • Stigmatized by speakers, but the origin of this stigma may be recent (Rodriguez Ordoñez 2013)

Distribution

Analysis of the distribution

  • There are two distinct areas of spread of DOM from Spanish speaking areas into Basque
    • The south
    • The west
  • This could mean that the two different types of DOM have different properties
    • More on this later…

Dative displacement (DD)

  • Movement of the dative agreement suffix to prefix position
    • Changing a nor-nori-nork form to nor-nork
    • (almost never/nowhere affects nor-nori verbs)
  • Formally parallel to (ubiquitous) ergative displacement in non-present forms

Example

Analysis

  • zibilak esan naute / biziro egoki
    […]
    giltzapean sartu naute / poliki poliki

    ‘The guardias civiles said to me very politely […]
    They slowly put me under lock and key’

  • The second line is a “canonical” use of the nor-nork paradigm for a monotransitive sentence
  • In the first line, the same nor-nork form is used even though the sentence is ditransitive
    • Canonical nor-nori-nork form is d-i-da-te

Distribution

Comments about the distribution

  • Spreading from Labourd
    • But other, unconnected outcroppings as well
  • Unlike for DOM, there is no obvious Basque-external source for DD
  • May be related to allocutive displacement, an innovation which occurs in Labourd (and High Navarre)
    • But has a different, less systematic distribution

Paradigm mixing

Caution against simplistic analysis

[DD] does not generally collapse the [nor-nori-nork] paradigm with the [nor-nork] paradigm for the same φ-features of dative and absolutive.

– Rezac (2008)

  • Despite the simplistic way I have introduced DD, it is not generally true that it can be described as substitution of one form for another

Pluralizer morphemes

  • Most of the time, the absolutive controls a plural agreement morpheme that immediately follows the root
  • This looks like “real” Agree-ment on T/v
    • Exactly one per finite clause
    • Always root-adjacent (but see below)
    • Can Agree across clause boundaries (Lafitte 1962 p. 223; Extepare 2003)

Pluralizer morphemes

  • For nor-nori-nork forms, the pluralizer is zki
    • eman dizut, eman dizkizut ‘I gave it/them to you’
    • Also occurs in non-auxiliary forms
  • In the nor-nork auxiliary, the pluralizer is slightly different
    • nau vs. gaitu
    • ninduen vs. gintuen
    • Confined to nor-nork auxiliary

Pluralizer morphemes and DD

  • In DD forms, it is controlled by the dative
  • The absolutive can control the ditransitive/synthetic pluralizer zki
  • The two can compete or coocur
    • gaitutzu (abs = sg) vs gaizkitzu, gaituzu (abs = pl; Biriatou)
    • compare gaituzkizu (elsewhere)

Syntactic differences

  • Rezac and Fernandez (2013) report syntactic effects of DD
  • In some Basque dialects only experiencer datives may agree with nor-nori auxiliaries, not goal datives
  • The variable-DD dialect of Oiartzun shows this pattern for non-DD forms
  • DD forms may agree with either kind of dative, however

Mutual influences

  • At the level of synchronic, syntopic morphosyntax, DOM and DD are separate constructions with their own rules
    • Neither is a “merely” morphological substitution
  • However, at the level of variation and change, they are mutually dependent and morphologically entangled

Map of both variants

Cooccurrence of DOM and DD

  • We might ask ourselves the obvious question: can DOM and DD cooccur in a single sentence?
    • Direct object → dative case (DOM) → absolutive(-like) agreement (DD)

Cooccurrence of DOM and DD

  • The answer is yes (Fernandez and Rezac 2010; 126–127)
    • Naturally occurring example: alde egin genuen inork guri ikusi ez gintuzen
    • Reports that such forms are becoming more common in Pasaia
  • But: a speaker from Lekeitio, which has DOM and DD separately, reports their combination is ungrammatical

Spread of pluralizer variation

  • Recall that (canonically) there are two different pluralizers for the auxiliary
    • it for nor-nork, zki for nor-nori-nork
  • However, there is cross-bleeding of these in various dialects

Conclusion

  • The pattern corresponding to the use of it in ditransitives overlaps very closely with the distribution of DOM
    • Specifically the Southern Navarrese DOM
  • In the presence of DOM, the nor-nork and nor-nori-nork paradigms collapse
    • Even though this is not waht DOM is, syntactically speaking
  • A similar pattern happens in reverse where DD “infects” the MT paradigm with zki

DOM again

Synthetic conjugations

  • So far weʼve only discussed auxiliary verbs
  • Synthetic verbs have no aspect marking, and tense/mood goes on the stem directly
  • An ancient, small, non-productive, and diachronically shrinking class
  • Data from Gamindeʼs study of Navarrese verbs (1985)

DOM in synthetic verbs

A surprising result

  • DOM occurs in synthetic verbs or auxiliaries, but not both
  • This is a very weird result
  • It must have something to do with how DOM spread
    • Which came first, the auxiliary chicken or the synthetic egg?

Variation, change, and grammatical analysis

Morpheme doubling and DD

  • In DD forms, the dative agreement can be double-marked by both a prefix and a suffix
    • Many Labourdin dialects zaizkizu etc.
    • Ahetze (Lab) and 2 Gipuzkoan dialects in Navarra gaituzkigu
    • Arcangues (Lab) hauk, haun
  • Does not seem to be a very robust option
    • Always co-occurs with variation
    • Almost always with plural absolutive (→ extra morpheme → processing effect?)

An analysis

  • Arregi and Nevins (2012) attempt to explain the difference between doubling and non-doubling in their (compendious) theory

Since doubling can only be the effect of a rule applying in the Linear Operations component, it cannot have an effect on agreement. On the other hand, a simple displacement can be the result of either [linear or non-linear operations] and can thus be optionally correlated with dative [A]greement. (309)

A critique

  • Doubling always occurs in DD-optional dialects; DD-mandatory dialects never have doubling
    • Limited evidence that DD-doubling is diachronically transitory
  • Doubling is also rare (in the context of DD)
  • Is it productive to chase down each and every possible variant within a single synoptic theory?
    • cf statistical concept of overfitting
    • cf literature on competing grammars in syntactic change

A counterexample

  • Convincing counterexamples to the A&N theory are difficult to find, because of the size of the dataset
  • Hereʼs the best I can do
  • Dialect of Urdiain (linguistically Gipuzkoan, geographically Navarrese)
  • DD with doubling for 1pl DAT with 3pl ABS
  • Auxiliary root is u for singular (multiple) Agree, and e for pl (???)

Conclusions

Lessons

  • Considering diatopic (and diachronic) variation is important to the study of synchronic morphosyntax
    • Variants which appear in the data may turn out to be artifacts of the spatiotemporal structure of the data
  • Even “bad” data is good
  • If you have spatial data, itʼs important to actually put it on a map
  • Dialect boundaries incubate changes
  • Basque is cool!
    • And thereʼs space for detailed dia- analyses
